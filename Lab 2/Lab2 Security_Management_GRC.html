1 Overview

The objective of this lab is to practise the security-management ideas from Topic 2: why security is a business and governance problem (not only technical), the GRC model (Governance, Risk, Compliance), relevant laws/standards/frameworks, building a risk register with response options, and business continuity planning (BCP) supported by a business impact analysis (BIA).

After completing this lab, you should be able to:

  • Explain why cybersecurity is a management and governance responsibility, not only a technical one.
  • Classify organisational actions as Governance, Risk, Compliance, or Awareness.
  • Match key laws, standards, and frameworks — including global privacy laws (GDPR, PDPA) and lawful bases for processing — to their main security or management implication.
  • Build a risk register, quantify risk with SLE/ALE, and choose appropriate risk responses (avoid, mitigate, transfer, accept, reject with evidence).
  • Run a BCP tabletop, reason about MTD/RTO/RPO and BIA, and identify the right continuity team.
  • Design a measurable security-awareness intervention and write a management-facing summary.

A1. Why is cybersecurity not only a technical problem?
Show suggested answer

Because management must decide ownership, funding, accountability, acceptable risk, legal duties, and continuity plans — decisions that technical controls alone cannot make.

A2. Give one example of a governance decision.
Show suggested answer

Making the CISO report to the board, approving a security roadmap, or defining security metrics.

A3. Give one example of a compliance obligation.
Show suggested answer

Protecting PII, keeping audit records, following PCI DSS for card payments, or meeting a legal requirement.

A4. What is PII, and why is it difficult to recover after exposure?
Show suggested answer

PII (personally identifiable information) is data that can identify a person, such as name, address, ID number, or account data. Once exposed it cannot easily be changed or recovered.

A5. Why are employees often called the weakest link in security?
Show suggested answer

Because phishing, mistakes, weak passwords, and poor reporting behaviour can bypass technical controls.

A6. What is an Authorization to Operate (ATO), and what does management demonstrate by issuing one?
Show suggested answer

An ATO is a formal management sign-off accepting the residual risk of a system before it goes live (“no ATO = no launch”). By issuing it, management demonstrates due care and takes accountability for the decision to operate — bridging technical security and business risk.

Short question. An online financial platform stores customer PII and processes card payments. Which two items from the table are most relevant, and why?
Show suggested answer

PCI DSS is relevant because the platform processes card payments. Privacy obligations (e.g. the Privacy Act or PDPA for customer PII) are relevant because it stores customer PII. ISO 27001 or COBIT may also be cited for management maturity and governance.

Global standard — lawful bases for processing personal data

Although the wording differs, most modern privacy regimes (EU GDPR, Malaysia PDPA, APEC) agree that personal data may only be processed when a lawful basis exists. The four most common are:

  • Informed consent — the individual actively agrees (GDPR requires explicit opt-in).
  • Legal / contractual — processing is needed to fulfil a contract or legal duty (e.g. KYC checks).
  • Vital interests — to protect someone's life (e.g. emergency medical access).
  • Public interest — for the “greater good” (public health, research).
Cross-border question. NeoBank onboards customers in both Malaysia and the EU. Which laws apply, and what is the “conflict of laws” risk it must manage?
Show suggested answer

PDPA applies to Malaysian customer data and GDPR applies to EU residents' data (GDPR reaches organisations outside the EU that serve EU residents). PCI DSS also applies because the platform processes card payments. The conflict of laws (“Catch-22”) risk: one jurisdiction may compel disclosure or data transfer that another jurisdiction forbids — so NeoBank can break one law no matter what it does. Mitigations: data localisation (keep EU data on EU servers), standard contractual clauses / approved transfer mechanisms, and legal review before cross-border transfers.

Quantitative risk: putting a number on it

The register above is qualitative (High / Medium / P1). Management often also wants the “math of risk” — a money value and a frequency — so controls can be compared on a cost–benefit basis. Use these formulas:

At today's weak MFA coverage, threat intelligence suggests such a breach is likely to succeed about twice a year. A proposed control — enforced MFA plus anomaly detection — costs RM 250,000 per year and is expected to cut the frequency to once every two years.

6 Part E: BCP Tabletop

Incident

NeoBank's identity verification provider is unavailable for 8 hours. New customers cannot complete onboarding. Existing customers can still log in and transfer money. A product launch is scheduled tomorrow.

Three recovery terms to know
  • MTD (Maximum Tolerable Downtime) — the longest a function can be down before the damage becomes fatal to the business.
  • RTO (Recovery Time Objective) — the target time to restore the service. RTO must be shorter than MTD.
  • RPO (Recovery Point Objective) — the maximum acceptable amount of data loss, measured in time (e.g. “at most 5 minutes of transactions”).

BCP vs DR: Business Continuity Planning keeps critical operations running during a disruption (the lifeboat); Disaster Recovery is the IT-focused subset that restores systems and data afterwards (toward the RTO and RPO).
